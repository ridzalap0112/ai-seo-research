## Key Themes

- SEO is becoming a broader search discipline. Mike repeatedly argues that the useful frame is not "classic SEO versus AI search" but search visibility across systems that have used AI for years and are now becoming more conversational, personalized, and answer-led.
- AI search makes visibility more persona-driven. He expects hyperpersonalization to make the idea of one shared SERP less reliable, which means content must be built for specific audiences, jobs, contexts, and decision paths.
- AI search requires omnimedia execution. Ranking only the owned website is not enough. Brands need visibility across review sites, earned media, LinkedIn, Reddit, social platforms, community surfaces, digital PR, and other sources AI systems can retrieve from.
- Measurement is the first major gap. Mike emphasizes share of voice, citation visibility, sentiment, brand impressions, business outcomes, and AI visibility rather than relying only on rankings and organic sessions.
- The SEO industry needs more experimentation and fewer opinions. He is openly skeptical of theory-only AI SEO commentary and wants data, case studies, working software, and repeatable testing.
- Automation will absorb much of traditional SEO execution. He describes many SEO workflows as ETL work: extract data, transform it, and load it into reports or recommendations. His view is that machines will automate much of that work.

## AI Search Insights

- AI search uses query fan-out. A visible user query can expand into a constellation of hidden synthetic queries, and brands need to understand which hidden queries influence the final answer.
- The winning unit is often a passage, not a page. AI systems retrieve and synthesize passages from candidate sources before producing an answer.
- Being indexed is only the starting point. Content must be accessible to conversational search systems, but it also needs to be selected, trusted, and synthesized.
- AI search has a recency bias. Mike suggests that generative search environments can respond quickly to new or refreshed content, with possible visibility movement in 30 to 60 days.
- Referral traffic will often be small. The value of appearing in AI answers may come from brand exposure, trust, and downstream demand, not direct click volume alone.
- AI search behavior varies by audience. Before building a Perplexity, ChatGPT, AI Mode, or AI Overview strategy, teams should verify whether their audience actually uses those platforms.

## Relevance Engineering Insights

- Relevance Engineering is a category for aligning SEO work with how modern retrieval and AI systems work. It is positioned as more technical, experimental, and system-aware than conventional SEO.
- The work starts with first principles: where information comes from, how it is selected, how passages are ranked or retrieved, and how reasoning chains synthesize final responses.
- Relevance Engineering is not only on-page optimization. It includes audience research, hidden query discovery, passage optimization, authority building, off-site source strategy, and measurement.
- The CMO-facing story is visibility in AI answers. Executives mostly want to know whether the brand appears when buyers ask "best," "who should I use," or category-level questions.
- Relevance Engineering requires experimentation inside brand constraints. Mike notes that a tactic like LinkedIn Pulse may work in some categories, but brands must choose channels they can realistically execute.
- The new scoreboard includes share of voice, citation visibility, sentiment, AI answer inclusion, branded and non-branded exposure, referral quality, and conversion behavior.

## Content Engineering Insights

- Content strategy must expand from website-only to omnimedia. The plan should include owned pages, earned media, reviews, communities, social posts, research assets, and potentially microsites.
- The goal is to create more candidate passages. Mike compares AI search visibility to a raffle: the more relevant candidate passages a brand has across trusted sources, the better its odds of being selected.
- Content needs audience dimensionality. One generic article for everyone is less likely to work in a hyperpersonalized environment; content should map to personas, contexts, and intents.
- Content refreshes and new deployments can matter quickly. Because AI systems often value recency, coordinated updates and new content pushes may produce faster learning loops than legacy SEO timelines suggest.
- Content should be machine-readable and machine-useful. The target audience now includes retrieval systems and LLMs, not only human readers.
- Human value moves upstream. If automation handles extraction, formatting, and basic optimization, human operators need to focus on strategy, original research, judgment, experimentation, and creative integration.

## Actionable Frameworks

### 1. AI Search Visibility Workflow

1. Measure current AI visibility across relevant platforms.
2. Identify where the target audience actually asks questions.
3. Discover hidden query fan-out patterns around priority topics.
4. Map the sources and passages AI systems cite today.
5. Build or improve candidate passages across owned and third-party surfaces.
6. Track citation visibility, sentiment, share of voice, and business outcomes.

### 2. Omnimedia Content Audit

1. Inventory owned website content.
2. Inventory off-site content: reviews, listicles, LinkedIn, Reddit, YouTube, analyst pages, partner pages, and media mentions.
3. Identify which sources AI systems cite for target prompts.
4. Check whether the brand appears in those trusted sources.
5. Fill gaps with digital PR, community work, refreshed content, first-party research, and structured comparison assets.

### 3. Candidate Passage Strategy

1. Start from a buyer prompt or AI-search question.
2. Infer the synthetic subqueries likely created by query fan-out.
3. Identify passage-level answers that would satisfy each subquery.
4. Publish or update assets so those passages exist in trusted places.
5. Test whether AI systems cite the passages and whether sentiment improves.

### 4. Measurement Model

1. Brand visibility: Does the brand appear in AI answers?
2. Citation visibility: Is the brand or its content cited?
3. Sentiment: Is the brand framed positively, neutrally, or negatively?
4. Share of voice: How often does the brand appear versus competitors?
5. Referral quality: Do AI-sourced visits convert?
6. Business impact: Does AI visibility correlate with pipeline, sales, or demand?

### 5. Experimentation Mandate

1. Give teams room to test platform-specific tactics.
2. Run short learning cycles instead of waiting six months.
3. Prioritize data and case studies over generic AI SEO claims.
4. Turn successful tests into playbooks.
5. Automate repeatable low-value workflows so experts can focus on strategy.

## Key Takeaways

- AI-powered SEO content production should optimize for retrieval, citation, trust, and synthesis, not just rankings.
- The practical unit of work is a trusted passage across the right source ecosystem.
- Brands need off-site authority and reputation because LLMs do not have to trust a company's own claims.
- Measurement must evolve from "rankings plus traffic" to AI visibility, citation visibility, sentiment, and business impact.
- The strongest AI search strategy starts with audience research and platform prioritization.
- Content teams need to build omnimedia plans that include owned, earned, social, community, and review surfaces.
- Relevance Engineering is a useful organizing concept for AI-era SEO because it connects technical retrieval mechanics to marketing execution.
- Automation will reduce the value of routine SEO tasks, making experimentation, software, research, and judgment more important.
